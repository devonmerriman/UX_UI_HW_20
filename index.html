The Problem

For the average user, the US Forest Service website is overwhelming and difficult to navigate.


The Solution

Our design approach was to focus on reorganizing navigation hierarchies and cutting back on page clutter, helping users focus on their goals when visiting the Forest Service website.


Research

To begin, my partner and I did a brand voice analysis to ensure the integrity of the Forest Service brand through our redesign. To do this, we devised a brand positioning statement to adhere to during our process:

The USDA Forest Service helps the American people maintain, utilize, and preserve national forests and grasslands by providing dependable and cooperative services that make the American people feel protected and provided for so they can enjoy and benefit from the natural abundance of their shared land for generations to come.

We also created a proto-persona we could keep in mind while designing a more user-friendly interface.


We then did a usability test with a sample size of 5 participants and told them to complete various tasks on the website. Among the complaints were:

  • Too many tabs in the primary and secondary navigation
  • Not enough visual information
  • Too wordy

Analysis

This data showed the need for a full rework of the information architecture. A heuristic evaluation backed up these findings, and we recorded them with redlining annotation:

With the combined results of the usability test, heuristic evaluation, and card-sorting, we created a new sitemap. We prioritized categories based on our user persona and cut down the primary navigation from 7 links to only 4.


Prototypes

Using the first draft of our wireframe, we gathered impressions of the desktop homepage with a 5 second survey, leading us to reevaluate the visual impression of the homepage:

  • Added a hero image to highlight the most used navigation feature
  • Utilized clear links to features, minimizing visual clutter
  • The testing also affirmed our direction for the general aesthetic.
We decided to use the tagline, "Your country, your forests" to bring a sense of ownership and connectedness to the user. A study from The Journal of Marketing further confirms our initiative to use possessive words on the homepage:


"Participants...were shown a sign at the park entrance that said either 'Welcome to the Park' or 'Welcome to YOUR Park.' Participants who saw the 'YOUR park' sign felt more ownership and responsibility for the park, were more likely to pick up trash, and would donate 34% more to the park.”


After conducting more user tests with our clickable prototypes, we decided to add a contact tab in the primary navigation. We also simplified the main navigation by limiting categories to primary and secondary tiers. This cleaned up any remaining navigational clutter and helped reinforce visual hierarchy with a new sidebar scheme for tertiary links.

Conclusion

We must do more user testing of our prototypes for further iterations. The tasks will be the same as our previous user tests to maintain reliability and consistency. We also want to design an easy-to-use, interactive map for users to search for a National Forest or Grassland.
